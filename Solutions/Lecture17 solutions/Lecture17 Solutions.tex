\subsection*{Question 1}
\begin{enumerate}[a)]
\item \quad \\[-1.45cm]
\begin{align*}
H_0:&\quad \text{The exponential model fits the data} \\[0.2cm]
H_a:&\quad \text{The exponential model does not fit the data}
\end{align*}
\item \quad \\[-1cm]
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|c|cccccc|c|}
\hline
&&&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
Class & $<$\,3 & 3\,-\,6 & 6\,-\,9 & 9\,-\,12 & 12\,-\,15 & $>$15 & $\sum$ \\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$o_i$ & 56 & 24 & 12 &  6 &  1 &  1 & 100\\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$p_i$ & 0.577 & 0.244 & 0.103 & 0.044 & 0.018 & 0.014 & \\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$100\times p_i = e_i$ & 57.7 & 24.4 & 10.3 & 4.4 & 1.8 & 1.4 & 100 \\[0.1cm]
\hline
\multicolumn{8}{c}{}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\item We need to combine the last three columns so that all expected frequencies are bigger than 5.
    \begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|c|cccc|c|}
\hline
&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
Class & $<$\,3 & 3\,-\,6 & 6\,-\,9 & $>$\,9 & $\sum$ \\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$o_i$ & 56 & 24 & 12 &  8 & 100\\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$e_i$ & 57.7 & 24.4 & 10.3 & 7.6 & 100 \\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$\frac{(o_i-e_i)^2}{e_i}$ & 0.050 & 0.007 & 0.281 & 0.021 & 0.359 \\[0.1cm]
\hline
\multicolumn{6}{c}{}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\item There are 4 frequencies in the above table and we estimated $\lambda$ to calculate the exponential probabilities $\Rightarrow$ $\nu = n_f -1 -k = 4 - 1 - 1 = 2$.\\[0.3cm]
    Thus, for the 5\% level the critical value is $\chi^2_{\,\nu,\,\alpha} = \chi^2_{\,2,\,0.05} = 5.991.$\\[0.1cm]
\item Since the test statistic $\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(o_i-e_i)^2}{e_i}  =  0.359$ is below the critical value, we accept the null hypothesis.\\[0.3cm]
    Conclusion: the exponential model fits this data.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{enumerate}[a)]
\item \quad \\[-1.45cm]
\begin{align*}
H_0:&\quad \text{The examiner and grade awarded are \emph{independent}} \\[0.2cm]
H_a:&\quad \text{The examiner and grade awarded are \emph{dependent}}
\end{align*}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|c|ccc|c|}
\hline
&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
Observed    & Examiner 1 & Examiner 2 & Examiner 3 & $\sum$ \\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
Grade A     &   9       &   6       &  10       &  25 \\[0.2cm]
Grade B     &  11       &  20       &  26       &  57 \\[0.2cm]
Grade C     &  25       &  19       &  74       & 118 \\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$\sum$        & 45 & 45 & 110 & 200  \\[0.1cm]
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

We need to calculate the expected frequencies using the formula: $e_{ij} = \frac{r_i\times c_j}{\text{total}}$.\\[0.4cm]
For example, $\frac{25(45)}{200} = 5.625$ corresponds to row one, column one.

\end{enumerate}

To calculate the test statistic we need the following:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|c|ccc|}
\hline
&&&\\[-0.3cm]
$\frac{(o_i-e_i)^2}{e_i}$    & Examiner 1 & Examiner 2 & Examiner 3 \\[0.1cm]
\hline
&&&\\[-0.3cm]
Grade A     &  2.02     &   0.02    &  1.02     \\[0.2cm]
Grade B     &  0.26     &   4.01    &  0.91     \\[0.2cm]
Grade C     &  0.09     &   2.15    &  1.28     \\[0.1cm]
\hline
\multicolumn{4}{c}{}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

$\Rightarrow$ $\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(o_i-e_i)^2}{e_i} = 11.77$.\\[0.5cm]
Note that $\nu = (n_r-1)\times(n_c-1) = (3-1)(3-1) = (2)(2) = 4$ and $\alpha = 0.05$ $\Rightarrow$ $\chi^2_{\,4,\,0.05} = 9.488$.\\[0.5cm]
The test statistic $\chi^2 = 11.77$ lies above the critical value $\chi^2_{\,4,\,0.05} = 9.488$ and, hence, we reject the hypothesis that there is no relationship between the two variables.\\[0.5cm]
Conclusion: The grade awarded depends on the specific examiner.
